01 — Microstructure

Hierarchical Structure

Nature's Nanoscale Engineering

The Tokay gecko (Gekko gecko) achieves wall-climbing through a hierarchical structure that works entirely without glue. Each toe bears setae — microscopic stalks 100µm long — which branch into spatulae, nanoscale paddle-shaped tips about 200nm across.

This branching architecture brings billions of atoms within 0.3nm of any surface, entering the range where van der Waals forces become significant. The system is completely dry, leaves no residue, and self-cleans with each step.

02 — Physics

Van der Waals Forces

The Physics of Molecular Attraction

Gecko adhesion runs on London dispersion forces — instantaneous dipole interactions between atoms. These forces fall off as r⁻⁶, making the separation distance the critical variable. At 0.3nm, the attraction is maximized. Beyond 1nm, it effectively vanishes.

The Lennard-Jones potential captures both repulsion (at very close range) and attraction (at optimal spacing). Spatulae geometry is tuned by 200 million years of evolution to maintain this sweet spot across virtually any surface.

08 — History

Discovery Timeline

From Aristotle to Nanotechnology

Aristotle's Observation
First recorded observation of gecko wall-climbing in Historia Animalium. The mechanism would remain unknown for 2,400 years.
~350 BC
1969
SEM Imaging
First scanning electron microscope images reveal the hierarchical setae structure, showing fine branching tips for the first time.
van der Waals Confirmed
Autumn et al. (Nature, 2000) definitively prove van der Waals forces — not suction or capillary action — drive gecko adhesion.
2000
2003
First Synthetic Gecko Tape
Geim et al. create first gecko-tape using carbon nanotube arrays, achieving 3 N/cm² adhesion — first proof of concept.
